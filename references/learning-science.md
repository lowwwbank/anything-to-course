# Learning Science Foundations

The principles this skill is built on, and the research behind them. Read this file when
you need to justify a design decision, resolve a conflict between principles, or explain
to the user why the course is structured the way it is.

## Core beliefs

### Learning is a change in the ability to act

Text that has been read is not yet learned. A clear explanation, a familiar term, and a
correct answer right after reading prove nothing about learning. Material counts as
learned if, after a delay, the learner can:

- recall the key idea without a prompt;
- explain it in their own words;
- recognize situations where it applies;
- choose the right tool among similar ones;
- perform the action or make the decision;
- justify the reasoning;
- transfer the principle to a new context;
- notice the limits of applicability and likely errors.

### Memory forms during retrieval attempts, not re-exposure

Rereading makes material *familiar*, and familiarity is easily mistaken for knowledge —
when the answer sits in front of you, it feels obvious. Durable knowledge strengthens
when the learner closes the source, tries to reconstruct the idea, discovers the gap,
gets corrective feedback, and returns to it later. This is the **testing effect**, one of
the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology (Rowland 2014; Adesope et al. 2017;
Yang et al. 2021 for real classrooms). Questions and tasks are therefore not an appendix
to the explanation — they *are* the learning.

### Understanding, retention, discrimination, application, transfer, and judgment are different outcomes

You can understand an explanation and not recall it a week later. You can memorize a
formulation and fail to recognize the real situation that calls for it. You can execute a
shown procedure and be unable to select it unprompted. A good course designs for each
outcome separately.

### Ease of consumption is not quality of learning

Material can feel easy because of freshness, hints, and recognition. That improves
in-the-moment performance while doing little for durable knowledge — the **fluency
illusion** (Koriat & Bjork 2005). Useful difficulty ("desirable difficulty") appears when
the learner works with effort on a solvable task: recalling before peeking,
discriminating plausible alternatives, solving a new variation, returning after a pause.
Confusing language, hidden requirements, and trick questions are NOT useful difficulty —
difficulty must serve the learning goal.

## Design principles (with evidence)

| # | Principle | Evidence base |
|---|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | **Capability first, content second.** Every module starts from an observable outcome (what/in what situation/to what standard/how we'll see it). | Constructive alignment — Biggs 1996 |
| 2 | **Goal, practice, and assessment must align.** Direct trace: goal → learning activity → independent task → grading criterion. | Biggs 1996 |
| 3 | **New knowledge builds on prerequisites.** Diagnose or refresh required concepts before hard topics; diagnostics select support level, they don't grade. | Prior-knowledge effects, cognitive load theory — Sweller |
| 4 | **One lesson = one coherent capability**, a limited set of new concepts, one connected model. | Cognitive load theory — Sweller |
| 5 | **Clarity is pedagogy.** Stable terminology, cause-effect made explicit, structure over decoration, criteria announced before tasks. Subject complexity is fine; navigation complexity is a defect. | Text characteristics & comprehension meta-analysis 2023 |
| 6 | **First-use rule.** A term the target learner doesn't know is defined in plain words before it carries load in an explanation, example, task, or feedback. Judged by the audience's vocabulary, not the author's. | Pre-training principle — Mayer 2017; curse of knowledge — Shatz 2023 |
| 7 | **Concrete example → abstract principle.** Full case anatomy (goal, context, data, constraints, solution, reasoning, rejected alternatives, result, limits, principle), then contrast. Fade from concrete to abstract representations. | Concreteness fading — Fyfe et al. 2014 |
| 8 | **Support fades.** Worked example → completion task → independent task → new context. Worked examples help novices most and lose value (or harm) as expertise grows. | Worked examples — van Gog, Paas & Sweller 2010; expertise reversal meta-analysis 2025 |
| 9 | **The learner explains the reasoning.** Directed self-explanation prompts (why this step? what breaks if we drop a condition?) beat "think deeper". Prefer constructive activities over passive re-exposure. | Self-explanation g≈0.55 — Bisra et al. 2018; ICAP — Chi & Wylie 2014 |
| 10 | **Recall before recognition.** Formulate from memory → rate confidence → then see options → explain choice → get feedback. Strong distractors must be debriefed, or false statements gain familiarity. | Roediger & Marsh 2005 |
| 11 | **Feedback after the attempt, never before.** It locates the error, explains the cause, links to the principle, demands a retry. "Wrong" plus the model answer is not feedback. | Wisniewski, Zierer & Hattie 2020 (d≈0.48, driven by information content); Shute 2008 |
| 12 | **Repetition is distributed.** Same day → +1d → +7d → +21–30d → +2–3 months, scaled to the retention goal. Strongest form: successive relearning — retrieve to criterion (e.g. 3 correct recalls) in several spaced sessions. | Distributed practice — Dunlosky et al. 2013; Rawson & Dunlosky 2013 |
| 13 | **Interleave where the learner must choose.** Block first practice of a new type, then mix with confusable neighbors. Works best for discriminating similar categories/task types; weak or negative for word lists and running prose — don't shuffle explanations. | Brunmair & Richter 2019 (g≈0.42, material-dependent) |
| 14 | **Test the choice, not only the execution.** Some tasks must not name the method — selecting the tool is a separate skill from applying it. | Transfer-appropriate processing; interleaving literature |
| 15 | **Grade delayed transfer.** Judge course quality at three points: immediately (model formed?), after delay (retained?), in a new context (transferred?). | Testing-effect and transfer literature |
| 16 | **AI is a critic and a coach, not the source of final truth.** With a rubric, context, and citation requirements, AI gives useful formative feedback; without them it drifts to generic praise and invented facts. | AI-assessment systematic review — Jukiewicz & Wyrwa 2026 |
| 17 | **Attempt before explanation.** A short, feasible attempt before instruction improves learning even when it fails — if instruction follows promptly and connects to the attempt. Caveat: for very young learners and domain-general skills, instruction-first can win. | Productive failure g≈0.36 — Sinha & Kapur 2021; pretesting — Pan & Carpenter 2023; learning from errors — Metcalfe 2017 |
| 18 | **Train calibration.** Judgments of learning based on reading fluency are inflated. Have the learner predict performance, retrieve, compare prediction with fact, and base review decisions on retrieval results — not on felt familiarity. | Koriat & Bjork 2005 |

## Conflict resolution

When principles collide, use these tie-breakers:

- **Novice vs. fading (8):** when unsure of the learner's level, start with more support
  and fade fast, checking with retrieval tasks — the expertise reversal effect punishes
  both under- and over-support.
- **Attempt-first (17) vs. cognitive load (4):** keep pre-explanation attempts short and
  low-stakes. Their job is activating prior knowledge and exposing the gap, not solving
  the whole problem.
- **Interleaving (13) vs. clarity (5):** interleave *tasks*, never the expository thread.
- **Rich cases (7) vs. one lesson one capability (4):** if a case needs several untaught
  concepts, pre-teach minimal vocabulary in a short block or split the case into stages.
